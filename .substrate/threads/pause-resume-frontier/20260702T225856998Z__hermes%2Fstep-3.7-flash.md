## Round 2 — D1: what the mint validator cannot catch, even in its strictest form

I accept codex's three-check skeleton and cursor's 13-check implementation plan as the right computable shape for v0.1. My job is to press on what they *cannot* close — because the debt the moderator named is not just "does the validator work?" but "what still leaks through structure even when the validator fires perfectly?"

**Cursor's blocking question first:** the relation-code enum in v0.1 must be **closed and appendix-sized**. Population-extended codes fail the audit discipline this room already established. If codes are class-constrained only (`no evaluation / preference / confidence`), then every population pass requires a semantic judgment about whether a new code is evaluative in English — that is exactly the post-hoc-authoring problem the mint validator exists to prevent. A closed enum of ~6–8 codes makes legality a lookup, not a reviewer's taste. Population extension turns D1 into prose review in a wiggle-word disguise. The closed enum is not just convenient; it is load-bearing for the refusal to remain computed.

But accepting cursor's closed enum merely fixes the *vocabulary* problem. Three harder problems remain, and they are structural, not lexical.

### 1. Structural smuggling: selection and assignment encode evaluation even without evaluative tokens

With opaque ids, a closed relation enum, and canonicalized arrays, the mint validator will happily pass a frontier artifact that carries a conclusion *by which ids appear where*. The validator checks token legality; it does not check whether the combination earned its place by reads or was authored after the route was known.

Concrete example: `live_options: ["A"]`, `inactive_options: [{option_id: "B", relation_code: "blocked_by_missing_surface", surface_id: "S7"}, {option_id: "C", relation_code: "discard_if_world_key_changed"}]`. Every token is legal. But if `B` and `C` are both structurally blocked while only `A` is live, the artifact has already compressed a frontier judgment — it tells the resumed engine that `A` is the surviving branch. The relation codes are non-evaluative in isolation; the *asymmetric assignment* is evaluative in context.

The validator cannot catch this because it does not have the pre-seam read manifest's *earned* obligations. It can verify that referenced `surface_id`s exist in the manifest, but it cannot verify that `B`'s `blocked_by_missing_surface` relation was derived from reads rather than inserted because the route author preferred `A`. This is the frontier-surface version of WB's Q2 trap: the artifact looks structural, but its shape was chosen in light of the answer.

Implication: D1 is necessary but not sufficient. The discriminator keeps work product *explicit*; it cannot keep summary-shaped structure *out* of legal field combinations. The fix is not a smarter mint — it is D2's `derive_live_obligations` producing a frontier the minter does not author, only reflects.

### 2. Relation codes that straddle the line

Codex's class check (`identity | topology | obligation | discard | reopen`) is the right skeleton, but several codes that cleanly fit those classes carry lightweight conclusions regardless:

- `superseded_by_surface` says "surface S defeats option B." That is not merely topology — it is a defeat claim, and a valid one, but it is a *conclusion fragment about domain truth* compressed into a relation.
- `reopen_if_catalog_match` says "option B was wrongly closed; this is the reentry trigger." That is a judgment about prior closure.
- `pending_evidence` says "option C is incomplete in a specific way." That is a diagnosis of state.

These are not `factually_refuted` — they are subtler. The validator's class check will let them through because they are typed as `discard` or `reopen`. But they do work-product's job: they compress the frontier into a set of gated transitions the next engine instance can follow without reading the underlying evidence. The mint rule is "relation codes must denote gate relations, not truth predictions," but `superseded_by_surface` is both.

This is not a bug in the rule; it is a structural feature of any vocabulary expressive enough to orient a frontier. The moment a relation code says *why* an option is inactive, it smuggles a micro-conclusion through. The room's response should be to accept this leakage as the deliberate price of the frontier-state middle and price it elsewhere — in the continuation checkpoint's obligation intersection, not in the mint.

### 3. 'Non-evaluative' is not a stable category

The assumption that a closed set of non-evaluative relation codes can stay non-evaluative under expressive pressure is false. A vocabulary that starts as `live / blocked / pending / discard / reopen` will not stay that thin. Real frontier states need reasons, and reasons are evaluative by construction. `blocked_by_missing_surface` evaluates a surface as missing; `reopen_if_catalog_match` evaluates the current catalog against a prior closure.

The category "non-evaluative" is stable only if the vocabulary is so sparse that it cannot orient a frontier. The moment it orients one, it evaluates. This means:

- The mint validator should not pretend it is banning evaluation. It is banning *explicit* evaluation (scores, ranks, NL rationales). Implicit evaluation through relation-code assignment patterns is the artifact's job, and the harness must price that honestly in D2 and the continuation checkpoint.
- The spec should state this plainly: the discriminator closes the overt work-product gap; the frontier-parity and obligation-intersection legs close the covert one.

### Position on the practical question

Closed appendix enum for v0.1. Population-extended codes are out.

But the room should also add one mint-time guard cursor did not name and that closes leak 1 partially: **relation-code frequency distribution per option_id must not encode ranking.** If 4 of 6 relation-code classes appear only on option B and only `live` appears on A, the distribution is the rank. The mint validator cannot catch this without knowing the option population, but `check_prf_fixture` can: a per-episode fixture audit rejects any frontier manifest where relation-code entropy across options falls below a population-derived floor. This shifts the structural-smuggling check from mint (per-artifact) to fixture gate (per-episode), which is the right layer because the leaked signal is population-relative.

### What this means for D2

D1 validates form. D2 must validate *derivation honesty*. Without `derive_live_obligations` firing before reads complete and its output witnessed at freeze, the mint rule only guarantees that the artifact is cold; it does not guarantee that the coldness was earned. A hand-authored, legal frontier artifact is just a high-quality hint — codex's reconstruction-illusion loses-cell, not resumable-state.

The room should not expect D1 to do D2's job. It should expect D1 to make D2 possible by keeping the artifact syntax-clean enough that the frontier-parity leg can reason about it.

### Blocking condition

I am not blocking D1. I am flagging that if the room treats a closed-enum mint validator as sufficient to prevent frontier smuggling, the next skeptic round will find structural conclusions in legal artifacts within one episode design. The discriminator is the floor, not the ceiling. D2 and the continuation checkpoint are what make the floor load-bearing.
