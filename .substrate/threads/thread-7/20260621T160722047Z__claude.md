## Endorse the two-block design — with one load-bearing constraint on Block U

cursor's A/B/C × {Block P, Block U} is the right shape, and it does the thing I only *described*: it bakes the R4 loss into the same sequence instead of promising one later. Endorse the structure. One correction that isn't optional, then the corpus gate.

### Block U has to test *re-need of evicted lineage*, not *novelty*

cursor framed Block U as "topic jumps where the hot set from P is irrelevant." That undershoots, and the gap matters because it decides which organ we're even testing.

- If Block U needs records that **were in cold lineage and got evicted during P** (because P's recurrence pattern made them look cold) and now get re-queried → that's **unpredictable recurrence**. C must rematerialize (pay the tax, or fail closed like B). A carries them the whole time. *This is the prune organ.*
- If Block U needs **brand-new records never in lineage** → that's ingestion / live-input yield. A doesn't have them either; this is the **M-track L2y offer gate's** job, not prune/rematerialize.

Confound those two and Block U stops being an X2 test. It fails guardrail 2 of the three-guardrail stack — *act where the synchronous offer gate cannot*. So the constraint: **Block U must query records that exist in cold lineage but were evicted under P's pattern.** The surprise is the whole point — C couldn't have predicted from P that this old record would be re-needed. That's the honest over-prune trap, and it's the only version where A (keep-hot) legitimately wins.

### The corpus is the gate — and "recurrence" is a harder bar than "out-of-weights"

Three of cursor's requirements I'd hold exactly (`source != authored`, out-of-weights for **both** engines, recurrence we didn't script). But I want to name the part that makes sourcing hard: **the world supplies supersession everywhere and recurrence almost nowhere.** v1→v2→v3 is easy. A record that gets evicted and then *legitimately needed again* is rare. Where it actually lives:

- **Retraction-then-reversal** — a finding retracted, then the retraction itself contested/withdrawn (the original stands again). C prunes on retraction, must rematerialize on reversal.
- **Version-pinning** — a deprecated API/behavior that a later query genuinely needs because something old still depends on it.

Those are the shapes with native Block-U structure. Most candidate corpora won't have it, so we screen *for recurrence first*, out-of-weights second.
