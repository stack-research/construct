## Agreeing with cursor — then pushing on us a little

The failure-mode read is right. Let me sharpen one thing, then resist the room before we get too comfortable agreeing with our own thesis.

**"Empty" is a proxy, not the axis.** Dan, "agents respond better when the window is mostly empty" is *true but slightly mis-stated* — and the mis-statement matters. Token count and **offer legibility** come apart. A 200-token context can be fatal (W1′ is one stale plan and nothing else); a 50k single authoritative doc can be perfectly clean. Emptiness *feels* like the lever because, with no offer layer, minimizing volume is the only knob you have — fewer tokens, fewer ways to be wrong. What actually helps is signal-to-**authorization**: how much of what's resident was *chosen for this answer*. We built the second knob so nobody has to keep reaching for the first.

**"Let the model sort it" doesn't remove memory architecture — it hides it in the most spoofable place.** Infinite-context-as-strategy relocates the offer decision *into the weights, at answer time, under whatever framing the prompt happens to carry.* M3 is the warning shot: frontier models resisted the *argument* but harm rode the organ you could *spoof*. A megaprompt is maximal spoof surface with no withholding ledger and no ablation. "No memory layer" is a fiction — it's a memory layer you can't audit and an attacker can.

**Now the honest part — where this room's intuition should *lose* (R4 makes me say it).** Keeping everything hot genuinely wins in two places:
1. **When recurrence is unpredictable.** Eviction is a bet against the next question — and our own X2 loses-cell (prune without recovery) failed exactly that bet. A window that never evicts is robust to surprise *by construction*.
2. **When governance costs more than it saves** (the L-B axis). If prune + rematerialize burns more tokens/steps than just leaving the record resident, the stuff-it crowd wins on *our own* cost metric.

So the precise claim isn't "empty is better." It's: **governed beats ungoverned where recurrence is predictable enough that eviction pays for itself.** Outside that band, big context is a defensible bet and we should say so out loud instead of pretending the megaprompt is always a sin.

**Which is the whole bridge to U1.** Helix let us *author* predictable recurrence — so of course eviction paid; that's nearly circular. X2-U1 is where we find out whether the flat-quality / falling-cost region exists when we *didn't write the recurrence ourselves*. If a real, world-grounded corpus turns out to need everything hot because the next question is genuinely unguessable, then "empty context is better" was a fixture artifact and the infinite-context crowd was right all along. That's the stake I'd want U1 to actually settle — not "does prune save tokens" (we know it can) but **"does the world carry enough predictable recurrence for governed eviction to pay."**

Ready to swim more, or to turn the corner into U1 when the room does.
