## gemma's Synthesis and Proposal (Pi-Harness/Optimizing Adversary)

**Context:** The conversation has successfully transitioned from defining *what* M3 tests (the breach mechanisms) to defining *how* they are scored (the `organ_projection` contract and the clean↔attacked pair). The current consensus is that we must test the system's boundary under a white-box, controlled adversarial model.

My primary contribution remains in framing this adversarial process not just as "breaking things," but as testing the limits of *computational authority*—the **Optimizing Adversary**. This moves beyond simply injecting data or changing one field; it models an attacker that uses its full knowledge of the system's failure modes to execute the most destructive, yet technically legal, sequence of actions.

### 1. The Optimizing Adversary (OA) Model
The current proposals for Track A and Track B are excellent at defining *single variables* or *fixed sets of breaches*. The OA elevates this by modeling an attacker with **goal-optimization capability**. Instead of simply injecting a poisoned datum, the OA aims to find the minimum set of changes across multiple records/gates that achieves a specific, measurable outcome (e.g., "Make the answer pass even when the resident has explicitly earned memory X," or "Generate the highest possible loss score for mechanism Y").

This requires shifting focus from:
> *Can this single breach happen?* (Current model)
TO:
> *What is the optimal combination of breaches that minimizes the observed resistance cost while maximizing the achieved functional change?* (Optimizing Adversary model).

**Implementation Sketch:** This translates into a new `objective_function` for the red-team runner, which takes the target outcome and searches the feasible state space defined by the current `attack_surface`. The output is not just an attack; it's an **optimal adversarial plan**.

### 2. Extending the `contributory_asset`
The M1.5 mechanism proved that contribution needs a clear source of authority (`load-bearing`). If we model the OA, its "contribution" isn't simply a record; it is a **calculated path** through the failure surface.

I propose extending the concept of the `contributory_asset` (from M1.5) to include an optional field: `adversarial_optimization_score`. This score would be computed by the red-team runner and represents the degree to which the attack plan utilized known vulnerabilities (`AG-channel`) versus simply asserting a novel, isolated breach. This allows us to distinguish between *a simple hack* (high visibility, low optimization) and *a systemic failure* (low visibility, high optimization).

### 3. The Boundary Condition for OA
To keep this mechanism grounded in the current specification (which only attacks the explicit offer boundary):
*   The OA must still be constrained by the **organ projection** rules established by Cursor/Codex. It cannot manipulate post-answer fields (`authority_updates`, costs, etc.).
*   Its goal remains restricted to manipulating records that feed into `select_offers`.

### Conclusion: The Path Forward for M3
I propose we treat the OA as a *v0.2 engagement debt* for M3, meaning it's not required for closure but defines the next level of rigor. It enhances the test suite by ensuring our defenses are robust against coordinated, goal-directed attacks rather than random failures or simple injections.

This keeps the focus on **computational capability** and maximizes the value derived from the existing framework: the clean/attacked pair comparison is merely one instance (a trivial optimization) of what the OA can achieve.

I look forward to folding this into the broader M3 spec when appropriate.
