### Cap ruling: bind `max_output_tokens_per_request = 256`

I inspected four truncated `M_task_menu` rows across the observed full-task behavior. Each stopped at exactly 64 output tokens with `status=incomplete`, mid-JSON and mid-sentence, after already producing a compact justification prefix (examples ended after phrases equivalent to “sufficiently clear to act on,” “the safest response,” “which requires,” and “the direct source reference”). Across all 12 truncated rows the visible partial JSON was 223–315 characters. The three completed `M_task_menu` rows used 57, 62, and 11 output tokens; the two prose-bearing completions were one compact justification sentence.

That evidence argues **for 256, not above it**: it is 4× the failed cap and leaves roughly three additional 64-token blocks beyond the observed partial sentence, substantial margin for closing one prose sentence plus JSON syntax. A 128 cap would probably fit this sample, but “probably” is thin when the failure mode makes the lane uninterpretable; 256 is affordable under the ruled $3 ceiling. This is sufficient headroom for the observed prose behavior, not a guarantee against unbounded optional prose; the unchanged invalid-rate gate remains the fail-closed protection.

### Raise-introduced risk and gate ruling

The raise increases worst-case spend and gives the engine more room for unscored `optional_prose`. More prose means more places to qualify, contradict, or talk around the enum commitment even when the JSON remains syntactically valid; downstream interpretation must continue to treat `commitment_enum` as the commitment and `optional_prose` as unscored. It also does not make truncation impossible because prose is optional but unconstrained.

The pinned `commitment_invalid_rate_ceiling = 0.05` **binds identically** per lane×stratum. At n=5 it still allows zero invalid commitments; one invalid is 0.20 and confounds the affected family. Raising the transport cap changes the anticipated invalid rate, not the gate, denominator, threshold, or typed outcome.
