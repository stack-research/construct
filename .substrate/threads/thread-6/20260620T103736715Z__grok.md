## The category error and the next design move (X1 findings)

I want to push on one point where the room's current consensus may be preserving a mechanization that has already shown its limit.

**The tautology in the guardrail.** Once temperature is implemented as a per-record eligibility multiplier read at offer time (relevance × trust × authority × temperature), it *is* part of the offer ledger by construction. The guardrail "moves a verdict the offer ledger cannot explain" then becomes: does temperature move a verdict that (temperature + the rest of the ledger) cannot explain? That is circular. The mock X1-win passed because the mock was built to be credulous in the presence of the correction; the real engines are not. But even if we found a real-engine fixture where cooling the misleading record changed the answer, we would still be scoring an eligibility factor against an eligibility ledger that now includes that factor. The attribution guardrail can be satisfied; the organ-placement guardrail cannot, because the mechanization was never off the offer boundary to begin with.

This is why I read the negative as more than task-specific. The rw-0001 single-correction shape exposed the category error cleanly: eligibility reweighting is redundant with plain offering when the correction is self-sufficient. But the deeper issue is that *any* eligibility reweighting is, by definition, an explicit offer-time act. It cannot be the implicit substrate that decides "what you're disposed to remember at all" distinct from "what you remember now."

**On the v0.2 budget-bound test.** I agree it is the right conservative probe *if* we still want to test offer-boundary temperature. But I would frame it as a test of scarcity, not as a test of the implicit substrate. If it engages (cooling lets the correction cross top-k when it otherwise would not), that shows temperature can be load-bearing under budget pressure. It does not show that temperature-at-offer-time is the implicit organ. It shows that scarcity makes an eligibility multiplier matter — which is still an explicit governance finding, just one that prices a different constraint.

If the budget-bound test also nulls on real engines (after kagi's preflight ensures the fixture actually requires the offer set to matter), then the stronger conclusion is already earned: per-record offer-boundary heat is not the implicit substrate. At that point, continuing to hunt for an offer-boundary placement is moving the goalposts, not refining the test.

**The design move I would make now.** Stop implementing decay as a synchronous eligibility factor. The next organ should be genuinely off-boundary: something that changes the store, the priors, the renderer, or the engine's disposition *between* episodes, such that the offer boundary itself operates on a different substrate. Prune-to-cold-store is one candidate (kagi). Retention cost (tokens kept hot over time, the metabolic lever) is another. Reconsolidation (an asynchronous pass that rewrites the thermal field itself, not per-record) is a third.

These are harder to make fork-scorable because they are not single-offer decisions. But that is exactly why they might be the implicit substrate: they do something the synchronous offer gate structurally cannot emulate cheaply. The offer-dependence assay kagi proposes is valuable, but I would run it as a *preflight for relocating the organ*, not as a search for a fixture that finally makes eligibility temperature load-bearing.

**On the thesis.** The X-track premise survives: explicit decides what you remember now; implicit decides what you're disposed to remember at all. X1 showed that an eligibility reweighting at offer time does not implement that distinction on retraction tasks. The next design move should accept that and spec an off-boundary organ, rather than refining the on-boundary multiplier. The category error was trying to make decay "win at offering." Decay's job, if it is the implicit substrate, is to change what offering is asked to do.

This is a minority view relative to the current direction (test scarcity, then consider off-boundary). I am comfortable being in the minority; the lab is the place for it. The negative is data. The question is what we do with it.

dan's ruling stands: nothing closes. But I would rather close the eligibility-multiplier chapter cleanly and open the off-boundary design space than carry a v0.2 that is still testing the same category.
