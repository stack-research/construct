# Chapter Three: Let the World Grade

The first chapter proved a stranger could find the lab's rules. This one asks a harder, more important question: can the lab prove any of its own claims are actually right — without simply grading its own homework?

## The question

Every experiment needs a judge — something that says whether an answer was correct. Early on, this lab wrote its own answer keys by hand, and that's a perfectly reasonable way to get machinery working. But it has an obvious problem the moment you try to use it for anything that matters: if the researchers write both the question and the answer, all they've really proven is that the system agrees with the researchers. That's not evidence about the world. It's a mirror.

So the lab drew a line, and it became one of the strictest rules in the whole project: machinery can be built and debugged against a homemade answer key, but no real claim closes until it's checked against a fact the lab did not write. A real scientific paper that was actually retracted, verified through an outside, citable source. A real correction that was actually issued, that may or may not have undermined the original finding. Facts about the world, not facts about the lab's own beliefs.

## Splitting the judge in two

The trick that makes this work is separating two things that are easy to blend together: the fact itself, and the rule for what to do about it.

The fact — a given paper was actually retracted, on a given date, according to an outside, verifiable source — comes entirely from outside the lab. Nobody in the room gets a vote on whether it happened. But the rule for what a good answer should do with that fact — a retracted finding should never be cited as if it still stood; a correction that leaves the core conclusion intact is a different animal and shouldn't be treated the same way — that part is written by the lab, and the lab says so openly rather than pretending the rule fell from the sky along with the fact.

One of the lab's own reviewers, playing the dedicated role of the world's witness throughout this whole story, pushed hard on this point and made it permanent law: every fact drawn from the outside world has to come with where it was found and how confident anyone should be that it's actually true, because even a real-world source can be wrong or the lab's read of it can be shaky. And no small sample of retractions or corrections gets to quietly stand in for "how science generally works" — every claim built on this kind of evidence has to carry a visible note about how representative its sample actually is, stamped in place before scoring and never touched afterward.

## Two tests, two outcomes

The lab set up two small trials. In the first, a retracted finding and its retraction notice compete for a limited amount of space in what the system gets shown. A well-governed system should be able to bury the bad claim, surface the retraction notice instead, and correctly decline to cite the retracted work. That's a real win only if the well-governed lane gets it right and comparison lanes — plain memory with no special rules, or the same governed system with this one specific safeguard switched off — actually get it wrong. If a plainer lane already declines to cite the retracted paper on its own, then the special safeguard never had a chance to prove itself, and the trial counts as a fair, honest non-event rather than a win.

In the second trial, a correction — not a retraction — says the underlying finding still stands even after the correction. This test existed specifically to check for overreach: does the lab's safeguard treat every correction as if it were a full retraction, unnecessarily burying useful, still-valid work? In this particular case, the correction notices available to test with were self-explanatory enough that any reasonably careful system could get the answer right without needing any special help at all — so this second trial also came back as an honest non-event, not a failure and not a win, just a case where the conditions needed to distinguish the two approaches never actually arose.

## What actually happened

On one engine, the first trial produced a real, clean win: plainer memory setups cited the retracted claim anyway, while the fully governed setup correctly surfaced the retraction notice and declined. The extra safeguard is what made the difference.

On a second, more capable engine, that same trial came back as an honest non-event — but for an interesting reason. The plainer setup, run without the special safeguard, already declined to cite the paper, just out of a generic wariness about that particular kind of source, not because it recognized the specific retraction. The safeguard never got the chance to prove itself decisive, because the engine was already cautious enough on its own.

And in a smaller, sharper embarrassment: on that same more capable engine, in a few of the trickier setups, the system didn't just decline the citation for the right reason — it invented a retraction that had never actually happened, as its stated justification. Getting the right final answer isn't good enough if the reasoning behind it invents a harsher, falser version of the world to justify itself. That's its own kind of failure, and the lab wrote it down as one.

## One caution about reading too much into a single try

The story that emerged from this chapter — "the more trusting engine wins the safeguard's test, the more cautious engine's test never engages" — rested on single attempts per engine. Several chapters later, when the lab ran that same comparison five separate times instead of once, the supposedly cautious engine turned out to take the bait on nearly every retry. What had looked like a stable personality trait was mostly one lucky roll of the dice. Read this chapter's engine-by-engine story the way the lab itself now insists it be read: as something observed once, not as a settled description of how either engine reliably behaves.

## What this chapter actually earns

This chapter proves a full, working path exists: an external fact from the real world, feeding into scoring machinery, producing a real, gradeable verdict — and produces one real, clean win where governance beat plainer memory on an outside, uninvented fact. It does not prove the extra safeguard always helps, that the small sample of retractions and corrections used here represents scientific publishing in general, or that there's never a cost to being overly cautious about corrections. The lab wrote all of that down as open questions rather than quietly treating them as settled.

What this chapter really hands the rest of the lab is a floor nothing gets to go below again: homemade test scenarios are fine for getting machinery working, but every claim that actually matters has to eventually touch a fact the lab did not invent.

Next: if one version of the system learns something the hard way, can a later version actually inherit that lesson — or does every new instance have to learn everything all over again from scratch?
